# Audience Communication Levels

How to match explanation depth to audience cognitive/educational stage.
Companion to `CONTENT-STYLE.md`, which covers tone and voice rules.

---

## Original 4-Level QuASAP

The formal QuASAP (Quality Assessment Scale from the Audience's Perspective)
defines four hierarchical levels. Peer-reviewed, empirically validated instrument
introduced in a 2024 Frontiers in Communication article. Multi-dimensional
quality assessment scale organized into four main quality levels, further broken
into 15 sub-dimensions (e.g., comprehensibility, credibility, fun, applicability).
Built through a seven-step development process and validated on a nationally
representative German sample.

| Level (QuASAP) | Typical Audience | Core Characteristics |
|-----------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Functional-system-oriented | Very young children or general public with no prior knowledge (e.g., kindergarten) | Pictograms, simple concrete analogies, vivid colors, single-sentence narrative; no technical terms. |
| Normative-democratic | Elementary-school learners (~5–10 yr) | Labeled diagrams, basic vocabulary, explicit statements of relevance; still highly concrete and story-driven. |
| User-action-oriented | Middle-school to high-school students (~11–16 yr) | Causal chains, simple formulas, "what-if" scenarios; audience asked to apply the idea to everyday situations. |
| Science-communication/format-specific | Undergraduate, graduate, and post-doctoral researchers | Full technical language, quantitative models, detailed methodology, citations, discussion of uncertainties; assumes familiarity with disciplinary conventions. |

The two ends:
- Level 1 — Functional-system-oriented (crayon drawings for kindergartners)
- Level 4 — Science-communication/format-specific (post-doctoral thesis style)

The three intermediate tiers are the normative-democratic, user-action-oriented,
and (sometimes split into) "undergraduate-intro" versus "graduate-deep-dive"
sub-levels, giving a total of seven distinct gradations when the two highest
levels are further subdivided for research audiences.

**Note:** While the core hierarchy (four levels) is the formal QuASAP model,
researchers commonly expand it into seven practical steps (the "expanded
QuASAP" used for curriculum design) by subdividing the two higher-order levels
(undergraduate-intro, graduate-deep-dive, post-doctoral) to reflect typical
educational milestones. This expanded version is not a separate standard — it
is simply a granular application of the validated QuASAP framework widely
adopted in science-communication research and teaching.

## Expanded 7-Level Scale

Most practitioners break the four QuASAP buckets into finer "steps" for
more precise tailoring. In the literature the QuASAP hierarchy is usually
expanded into seven widely-used sub-levels (or even ten in some variants).

| Level | Name | Typical Audience | Communication Style |
|-------|------|------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Crayon / Visual | Kindergarten (~5 yr) | Single-panel drawings, bright colors, one-word labels ("rain", "bird"). No sentences. |
| 2 | Picture-book | Early elementary (~6–8 yr) | Simple story-line, labeled illustrations, 1–2 short sentences per page. |
| 3 | Classroom-board | Upper elementary (~9–11 yr) | Basic cause-effect diagram, everyday analogies, occasional simple equation (e.g., speed = distance ÷ time). |
| 4 | High-school primer | Teens (~12–16 yr) | Formal definitions, multiple-step reasoning, "what-if" scenarios, short paragraph explanations. |
| 5 | Undergraduate intro | College freshman (~18 yr) | Full terminology, derivations of core formulas, real-world data examples, brief literature citations. |
| 6 | Graduate deep-dive | Master's/PhD (~22–30 yr) | Complete mathematical treatment, extensive references, discussion of assumptions, limitations, and alternative models. |
| 7 | Post-doctoral / expert | Researchers, specialists | Cutting-edge methodology, rigorous proofs, exhaustive bibliography, future-research directions, reproducibility details. |

**Two ends:**
- Level 1 — purely visual, intuitive (crayon drawings for kindergartners)
- Level 7 — dense technical prose, full scholarly apparatus (post-doctoral thesis)

**Why 7?** The first three steps are visual-concrete, the next two are
educational-progressive, and the last two capture research-level rigor. This
mirrors how students naturally move from concrete to abstract thinking, and it
aligns with cognitive-development research (Piaget's stages) as well as the
Bloom taxonomy levels of learning (Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze →
Evaluate → Create).

## Complementary Grading Systems

These add granularity within each QuASAP level.

| System | Maps to QuASAP as | Step Count |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| Bloom's Revised Taxonomy | Cognitive verb per level (Identify → Step 1, Explain → Step 3, Analyze → Step 5, Synthesize → Step 7) | 6 levels (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create) |
| Flesch-Kincaid readability | Numeric grade-level (0–12+) within each step to tune sentence complexity | Continuous (0–12+) |
| ICAP framework (Interactive-Constructive-Active-Passive) | Engagement type per level (Passive for 1–2, Active for 3–4, Constructive for 5–6, Interactive for 7) | 4 categories |
| Science-Communication Ladder (SCL) by National Academies | Similar 5-step ladder (public, lay-intermediate, STEM-undergrad, graduate, professional). Often split into 10 sub-steps for curricula design | 5–10 levels |

### Multi-Dimensional Matrix

| Dimension | Example Values |
|-----------|----------------|
| Depth (QuASAP) | 1–7 |
| Cognitive demand (Bloom) | Remember → Create |
| Readability (Flesch-Kincaid) | Grade 2 → Grade 12 |
| Engagement type (ICAP) | Passive → Interactive |

### Putting It Together in Practice

1. Pick the target audience → identify their typical education level.
2. Select a QuASAP step that matches the needed depth.
3. Adjust readability with Flesch-Kincaid to hit the exact grade-level you want.
4. Choose a Bloom verb to set the learning objective (e.g., "Explain" for Step 4, "Analyze" for Step 6).
5. Design activities that hit the corresponding ICAP engagement level (e.g., simple labeling for Step 1, data-analysis exercise for Step 6).

This stacked approach gives seven concrete depth levels plus the flexibility
to slice each level into finer increments, ensuring that a complex scientific
idea can be presented exactly where the audience's cognitive and educational
readiness meets the communication goal.

## Your Target Levels

Define which QuASAP levels apply to this KB's output. Replace this section
with your own target levels — the framework above stays the same regardless
of domain or format.

For each level, specify:

- **Audience profile** — education level, assumed prior knowledge
- **Vocabulary standard** — what jargon is allowed and how to handle it
- **Sentence length** — average word count per sentence
- **Evidence expectations** — headline stat vs. full citation with author/year
- **Analogy use** — frequency and function (bridging vs. illustrative)
- **Typical output length** — word count or time equivalent

### Example: Level 4 — High-School Primer

| Aspect | Guidelines |
|--------|------------|
| Audience | General adult, high-school education |
| Sentence length | 12–20 words avg.; one claim per sentence |
| Evidence | One data point or headline stat per claim |
| Jargon | Define inline (≤ 5 words) or avoid |
| Analogy | At most one per piece |
| Typical length | 300–400 words |

### Example: Level 5 — Undergraduate Intro

| Aspect | Guidelines |
|--------|------------|
| Audience | Well-read non-specialist, ready for modest technical depth |
| Sentence length | 18–25 words avg. |
| Evidence | 2–3 citations with author/year |
| Jargon | Define on first use (≤ 10 words) |
| Analogy | One per major claim (2–3 total) |
| Typical length | 1,200–1,500 words |

Replace or extend these examples with the levels that match your KB's output
format and audience.
